Design

Introduction

Curli fibers are extracellular amyloid proteins naturally produced by E. coli that self-assemble into strong fibrous networks. Since these are secreted outside the cell and remain stable after cell lysis, they are an ideal biological scaffold. In this project, we aimed to engineer curli fibers to bind effectively with MXene which is a negatively charged two-dimensional nanomaterial, and to integrate them into a composite filtration system for the removal of organochlorine pesticides (OCPs) from water.

To enhance interactions between curli fibers and MXene, we genetically modified the major curli subunit protein CsgA by adding a C-terminal poly-lysine tag (K8). Lysine residues are positively charged at physiological pH, enabling strong electrostatic attraction to the negatively charged surface terminations of MXene.

Construct Design (pET-22b–csgA)

The pET-22b(+) expression vector was used to construct a plasmid encoding the csgA gene under the control of a T7 promoter for inducible expression in Escherichia coli. This vector was selected due to its strong, well-characterized T7-based expression system and compatibility with downstream protein purification.

Genetic Elements of the Construct

The final construct consists of the following elements in order:

  1. T7 Promoter:
    Drives high-level transcription in E. coli strains expressing T7 RNA polymerase.
  2. Ribosome Binding Site (RBS):
    Ensures efficient translation of the downstream gene.
  3. csgA Coding Sequence:
    Encodes CsgA, the major structural subunit of curli amyloid fibers.
  4. C-terminal 6×His Tag:
    Fused in-frame to csgA to enable purification via nickel affinity chromatography (Ni-NTA).
  5. T7 Terminator:
    Signals proper termination of transcription and improves mRNA stability.
  6. Ampicillin Resistance Gene (AmpR):
    Provides antibiotic selection to identify cells carrying the plasmid.
  7. ColE1 Origin of Replication:
    Allows stable replication of the plasmid in E. coli.

Gibson Assembly Procedure for pET-22b–csgA

The csgA gene was assembled into the pET-22b(+) vector using Gibson Assembly. The insert was designed in Benchling with homologous overlaps to the vector multiple cloning site (MCS), ensuring correct orientation and in-frame fusion with the C-terminal 6×His tag.

Fragment Design (Benchling)

Two DNA fragments were used:

  1. pET-22b(+) Backbone:
    The vector was linearized in silico at the MCS region and retained:
    • T7 promoter
    • Ribosome binding site
    • C-terminal 6×His tag
    • T7 terminator
    • Ampicillin resistance gene (AmpR)
    • ColE1 origin of replication
  2. csgA Insert:
    The csgA coding sequence was PCR-amplified. Primers were designed in Benchling to introduce:
    • 5′ overlap homologous to the vector region upstream of the MCS
    • 3′ overlap homologous to the region immediately upstream of the 6×His tag

These homologous overlaps enabled seamless Gibson Assembly. To ensure that the csgA insert remained in-frame with the plasmid, two additional nucleotides (T and A) were introduced immediately after the MCS. This prevented a frameshift at the vector–insert junction and preserved the correct reading frame, allowing continuous translation from the upstream start codon through the csgA coding sequence, beginning with its N-terminal methionine.

Benchling’s translation and frame visualization tools were used to confirm correct amino acid translation across the vector–insert junction.

In Silico Gibson Assembly

Benchling’s Gibson Assembly tool was used to:

  • Simulate assembly of the linearized pET-22b(+) backbone with the PCR-amplified csgA insert
  • Confirm correct orientation and junction sequences
  • Verify that the final plasmid contained all intended genetic elements in the correct order

Following in silico design and Gibson Assembly, the assembled pET-22b–csgA plasmid will be transformed into an Escherichia coli K-12 strain for cloning and plasmid propagation. K-12 strains were selected due to their genetic stability and lack of T7 RNA polymerase, preventing unintended expression of csgA during the cloning stage. Transformants will be selected on ampicillin-containing agar and used for downstream verification.

Proposed Approach Using Synthetic Biology

Our project uses the Biofilm-Integrated Nanofibre Display (BIND) strategy to engineer functional biofilm materials using the native curli system of Escherichia coli. BIND enables the extracellular display of genetically encoded peptide domains by fusing them to CsgA, the major structural subunit of curli amyloid fibres. Through this approach, biofilm formation and material functionality are genetically programmed at the protein level (Nguyen et al., 2014;Sivanathan & Hochschild, 2012;Chen et al., 2014;Botyanszki et al., 2015).

BIND Mechanism- (Biofilm Integrated Nanofibre Display)

Engineering Rationale

Curli fibres are extracellular amyloid nanofibres naturally produced by E. coli and form the structural scaffold of bacterial biofilms. The curli biogenesis pathway secretes unfolded CsgA monomers through the outer-membrane pore CsgG, where they assemble extracellularly into fibres nucleated by CsgB. CsgA tolerates the fusion of short, unstructured peptide domains without disrupting secretion or fibre assembly.

The BIND strategy exploits this tolerance by genetically fusing functional peptides to CsgA, thereby enabling simultaneous fibre assembly and functional display within the biofilm extracellular matrix.

This BIND-based design enables material functionality to be encoded directly in the genetic sequence, eliminates the need for external modification steps during fiber formation. A lysine-rich region ensures better binding and stability. The resulting system converts bacterial biofilms into living, self-assembling materials with genetically programmable properties (Nguyen et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014).

Mechanism of BIND in Our System:

  1. Genetic fusion of a lysine-rich peptide domain to CsgA using a flexible linker.
  2. Expression of the protein and secretion through the natural curli export system.
  3. Extracellular self-assembly of the monomers into amyloid nanofibres on the cell surface nucleated by CsgB.
  4. Display of the lysine-rich domains at high density on the surface of the fibre network and transformation of the biofilm matrix into a programmable material network.

Conclusion

By integrating lysine-functionalized peptides into curli nanofibres using the BIND strategy, our engineering approach transforms E. coli biofilms into genetically programmable material systems. This design establishes a robust and modular platform for engineering biofilm-based functional materials.

Filtration System for Removal of Organochlorine Pesticides (OCPs)

It has a three-component structure consisting of chitosan beads, nanosheets of MXene materials, and curli fibers that remove organochlorine pesticides effectively from polluted water. The chitosan bead layer functions as the main medium for adsorption. Chitosan, which is found in chitin, has amine and hydroxyl groups, which allow for electrostatic attraction and hydrogen bonding between the chitosan and the hydrophobic pesticide, hence being very efficient in the removal of organochlorines from water (Lu et al., 2011). Chitosan powder is readily available and can be purchased from many sources such as IndiaMART. The beads can then be prepared in the lab.
The layer of MXene nanosheets provides a high surface secondary filtration and catalysis layer. Generally, MXenes possess hydrophilic, conductivity, and surface chemistry attributes, and their addition to protein matrices, as has been shown in some recent bioprinting studies, provides mechanical strength and antibacterial capability (Garcia et al., 2024). The MXene can be procured from Sigma Aldrich.
The curli fiber layer, made of self-assembling bacterial amyloid proteins, is a nanofibrous network which increases mechanical filtration properties and also increases the number of binding sites due to amino acid functional groups. The curli fiber increases biofilm stability and also increases the binding of residual pesticide molecules and nanoparticles.
This multi-functional filtration technique combines physical sieving action, chemical adsorption processes, and biological/nanomaterial interactions in an effort to remove organochlorines from water. However, due to the strict regulation of OCPs (high toxicity), we intend to use an analog, pentachlorocyclohexane, during our testing process.
