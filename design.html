Design

Introduction

Curli fibers are extracellular amyloid proteins naturally produced by E. coli that self-assemble into robust fibrous networks. Because curli fibers are secreted outside the cell and remain stable after cell lysis, they are an ideal biological scaffold for creating non-living, functional biomaterials. In this project, we aimed to engineer curli fibers to interface effectively with MXene, a negatively charged two-dimensional nanomaterial, and to integrate them into a composite filtration system for the removal of organochlorine pesticides (OCPs) from water.

To enhance interactions between curli fibers and MXene, we genetically modified the major curli subunit protein CsgA by adding a C-terminal poly-lysine tag (K8). Lysine residues are positively charged at physiological pH, enabling strong electrostatic attraction to the negatively charged surface terminations of MXene.

Genetic Construct Design

The engineered construct was designed with the following architecture:
PcsgA → RBS → csgA → GSGSGS linker → K8 tag → BBa_B0015 terminator

  • PcsgA promoter:
    The native curli promoter was used to ensure expression under curli-inducing conditions, maintaining physiological relevance and minimizing metabolic burden.
  • Ribosome Binding Site (RBS):
    A standard bacterial RBS was included to ensure efficient translation initiation.
  • csgA coding sequence:
    Encodes the major structural subunit of curli fibers.
  • GSGSGS flexible linker:
    A short glycine-serine-rich linker was inserted to spatially separate CsgA from the lysine tag, reducing steric interference and preserving proper curli fiber assembly.
  • K8 poly-lysine tag:
    Eight lysine residues were appended to the C-terminus of CsgA to enhance electrostatic binding to MXene. K8 was chosen to increase surface charge while minimizing risks of aggregation or secretion defects that can occur with longer poly-lysine sequences.
  • BBa_B0015 terminator:
    A standard double terminator was included to ensure reliable transcriptional termination and prevent read-through into downstream sequences.

Gibson Assembly and Transformation strategy:

The final engineered plasmid, pSB1C3_PcsgA_CsgA-K8, should encode a lysine-functionalized curli subunit that can self-assemble into fibers that are capable of linking with MXene. This design would help produce a cell-free component that can be integrated with chitosan and chitin into composite filtration films for environmental remediation.
Gibson Assemble can be used as a cloning technique here. In this approach, the curli expression cassette (PcsgA-csgA-K8-BBa_B0015) and the pSB1C3 backbone will be amplified using PCR with the help of primers, introducing overlapping homologous regions. These fragments will then be assembled in a single isothermal reaction that will ensure its precise and efficient construction.
Now, the recombinant plasmid can be introduced into the competent Escherichia coli DH5α cells via heat-shock transformation. DH5α has a high transformation efficiency and suitability for plasmid propagation and sequence verification. Positive clones will be identified through antibiotic selection and further confirmed by restriction analysis and sequencing. Then the plasmid will be transformed into a curli-producing E. coli K-12 strain for downstream curli fiber expression and other functional assays.

Proposed Approach Using Synthetic Biology

Our project employs the Biofilm-Integrated Nanofibre Display (BIND) strategy to engineer functional biofilm materials using the native curli system of Escherichia coli. BIND enables the extracellular display of genetically encoded peptide domains by fusing them to CsgA, the major structural subunit of curli amyloid fibres. Through this approach, biofilm formation and material functionality are genetically programmed at the protein level (Nguyen et al., 2014;Sivanathan & Hochschild, 2012;Chen et al., 2014;Botyanszki et al., 2015).

BIND Mechanism- (Biofilm Integrated Nanofibre Display)

Engineering Rationale

Curli fibres are extracellular amyloid nanofibres naturally produced by E. coli and form the structural scaffold of bacterial biofilms. The curli biogenesis pathway secretes unfolded CsgA monomers through the outer-membrane pore CsgG, where they assemble extracellularly into fibres nucleated by CsgB. CsgA tolerates the fusion of short, unstructured peptide domains without disrupting secretion or fibre assembly.

The BIND strategy exploits this tolerance by genetically fusing functional peptides to CsgA, thereby enabling simultaneous fibre assembly and functional display within the biofilm extracellular matrix.

This BIND-based design enables material functionality to be encoded directly in the genetic sequence, eliminating the need for external modification steps during fiber formation. By using lysine-rich peptide domains, we introduce tunable surface chemistry while preserving robust curli assembly and biofilm formation. The resulting system converts bacterial biofilms into living, self-assembling materials with genetically programmable properties (Nguyen et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014).

Mechanism of BIND in Our System:

  1. Genetic fusion of a lysine-rich peptide domain to CsgA using a flexible linker.
  2. Expression of the protein and secretion through the natural curli export system.
  3. Extracellular self-assembly of the monomers into amyloid nanofibres on the cell surface nucleated by CsgB.
  4. Display of the lysine-rich domains at high density on the surface of the fibre network and transformation of the biofilm matrix into a programmable material network.

Conclusion

By integrating lysine-functionalized peptides into curli nanofibres using the BIND strategy, our engineering approach transforms E. coli biofilms into genetically programmable material systems. This design establishes a robust and modular platform for engineering biofilm-based functional materials.

Filtration System for Removal of Organochlorine Pesticides (OCPs)

It has a three-component structure consisting of chitosan beads, nanosheets of MXene materials, and curli fibers that remove organochlorine pesticides effectively from polluted water. The chitosan bead layer functions as the main medium for adsorption. Chitosan, which is found in chitin, has amine and hydroxyl groups, which allow for electrostatic attraction and hydrogen bonding between the chitosan and the hydrophobic pesticide, hence being very efficient in the removal of organochlorines from water (Lu et al., 2011). Chitosan powder is readily available and can be purchased from many sources such as IndiaMART. The beads can then be prepared in the lab.
The layer of MXene nanosheets provides a high surface secondary filtration and catalysis layer. Generally, MXenes possess hydrophilic, conductivity, and surface chemistry attributes, and their addition to protein matrices, as has been shown in some recent bioprinting studies, provides mechanical strength and antibacterial capability (Garcia et al., 2024). The MXene can be procured from Sigma Aldrich.
The curli fiber layer, made of self-assembling bacterial amyloid proteins, is a nanofibrous network which increases mechanical filtration properties and also increases the number of binding sites due to amino acid functional groups. The curli fiber increases biofilm stability and also increases the binding of residual pesticide molecules and nanoparticles.
This multi-functional filtration technique combines physical sieving action, chemical adsorption processes, and biological/nanomaterial interactions in an effort to remove organochlorines from water. However, due to the strict regulation of OCPs (high toxicity), we intend to use an analog, pentachlorocyclohexane, during our testing process.
