Description

Background

Among the class of persistent chemical pollutants, the organochlorine pesticides (OCPs), which were widely applied for agricultural pest control and public health programs during the last century, have been widely used in India. Because of their high chemical stability and persistence, several OCPs remain in the environment for many years after they are no longer used, as they have been banned in India. The residues of OCP have been detected in soil, surface water, groundwater, sediments, and food products from different parts of India (mainly agricultural areas) by several workers. In terms of volume, OCPs account for 40% of pesticide consumption, followed by organophosphates at 30%, carbamates at 15%, and synthetic pyrethroids at 10%, with the remaining 5% spread across various other categories. OCP consistently exhibits the highest levels among these groups, with its presence detected across multiple river sites (Behera et al., 2024) .


Problem Statement

The widespread use of chemical pesticides has resulted in extensive contamination of India's water bodies, with organochlorine pesticides (OCPs) representing a key group of persistent and toxic pollutants. OCPs, including DDT, lindane, endosulfan, aldrin, dieldrin, and chlordane, are among the oldest and most environmentally stable pesticides that continue to persist in aquatic ecosystems due to their resistance to degradation . These compounds were detected in major Indian rivers such as the Ganges, Yamuna, Cauvery, and Gomti, primarily through agricultural runoff and leaching from application sites into surrounding water systems (Behera et al., 2024).

The presence of OCPs in the biosphere remains unbroken. Some of the most common OCPs have been found to persist in soils and sediments for 5 to 30 years or more (Sharma et al., 2019) . Because of their physicochemical properties, organochlorine residues readily bind to sediments and organic matter, enabling long-term persistence and potential bioaccumulation in aquatic food webs. This persistence increases ecological and health risks as these toxic residues can travel through ecosystems and enter drinking water sources. The presence of OCP residues across different river basins and notes that these chemicals bioaccumulate, disrupt ecological balance, and pose hazards to fauna and human populations reliant on contaminated water.

Existing water treatment and environmental degradation pathways are insufficient to remove these recalcitrant compounds effectively. Their stability and low degradability mean that natural depletion alone does not eliminate them from contaminated water bodies, emphasizing the need for targeted pollutant capture and removal strategies (Behera et al., 2024).

Our Solution

Persistent pesticides require persistent solutions; to address the problem of organochlorine pesticides (OCPs) in water, we propose a Bio-Derived modular adsorption filter designed for the removal of OCPs from pre-treated water, rather than their chemical degradation. OCPs are chemically stable and extremely hydrophobic, making total breakdown challenging and unfeasible (Rios-Fuster et al., 2021).

Instead of degrading these compounds, our approach is to physically remove them from water, reducing their bioavailability and environmental dispersion. Our system combines chemical, biological, and nanomaterial elements into a single modular filter. In our solution, curli fibers are produced biologically and then extracted for use as a non-living material scaffold. They are naturally secreted extracellular protein fibers produced by E. coli, exhibit mechanical properties, and have been shown to interact with a range of surfaces and nanomaterials. We will incorporate MXene nanosheets to enhance surface interactions and adsorption capacity through their layered structure and tunable surface chemistry (García et al., 2024). Moreover, MXene nanosheets have antibacterial properties (Meskher et al., 2024). Now, we introduce Chitosan beads as a pollutant-binding phase utilising functional groups and hydrophobic interactions to bind organochlorine compounds (Lu et al., 2010). These components work together to create a composite adsorption filter capable of capturing OCPs from water that has already undergone primary treatment.

The developed method is non-destructive and aimed at safe extraction of organochlorinated pesticides. It is a non-living and biosafe system, making it suitable for environmental deployment without introducing live organisms. The modular design allows individual material components to be tuned or replaced based on specific application needs, while its scalability and field-relevance make it particularly appropriate for decentralized, resource-limited water treatment settings. By selectively targeting hydrophobic pesticides, this hybrid filter offers a practical and sustainable strategy for managing legacy organochlorine contamination in India.
