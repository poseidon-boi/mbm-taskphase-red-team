Congo Red Staining for Curli Fiber Detection

Aim

To detect and confirm the presence of curli amyloid fibers produced by engineered E. coli using Congo Red staining, based on the dye’s affinity for β-sheet–rich amyloid structures (Chapman et al., 2002; Barnhart & Chapman, 2006).

Principle

Curli fibers are extracellular functional amyloids composed primarily of the CsgA protein. Amyloid structures exhibit a characteristic cross-β sheet conformation that binds Congo Red, resulting in visible red staining. Qualitative Congo Red staining is therefore widely used to confirm curli production in E. coli (Hammer et al., 2007).

Materials and Reagents

Equipment

  • Clean glass microscope slides
  • Light microscope
  • Slide rack
  • Heat source for fixation

Reagents

Congo Red solution (aqueous, low-percentage), distilled water or PBS (physiological pH), and cell suspension of curli-expressing E. coli or isolated curli fibers.

Preparation of Curli-Producing Cell Suspension

Engineered E. coli strains carrying the curli expression construct are cultured under curli-inducing conditions. Cells are harvested, washed to remove residual media, and resuspended in aqueous solution to obtain a homogeneous suspension containing surface-associated curli fibers.

  1. Sample Preparation

    A small volume of the curli-producing cell suspension is spread on a clean glass slide, air-dried, and gently heat-fixed if required (Barnhart & Chapman, 2006).

  2. Staining

    The dried smear is covered with aqueous Congo Red solution and incubated at room temperature to allow selective binding to β-sheet-rich amyloid structures (Chapman et al., 2002).

  3. Washing and Observation

    Excess dye is gently rinsed off with distilled water. After air-drying, the sample is examined under a light microscope.

Expected Observation:
Red staining → Presence of curli amyloid fibers
Minimal or no staining → Absence of curli fibers

Extraction of Extracellular Lysine-Tagged Curli Fibers

Aim

To extract extracellular curli fibers without depolymerization using detergent washes and differential centrifugation (Garcia et al., 2024).

Principle

  • High β-sheet content
  • Resistance to detergents
  • Insolubility under native conditions

These properties allow selective purification via mechanical shearing and high-speed centrifugation.

Materials Required

Buffers and Reagents

  • SDS (2% w/v)
  • DNase I (10 µg/mL)
  • RNase A (10 µg/mL)
  • Proteinase K (20 µg/mL, optional)
  • Deionized water
  • Optional: 6 M guanidine HCl or 90% formic acid

Equipment

  • Refrigerated centrifuge (6,000–10,000 × g)
  • Ultracentrifuge (100,000 × g)

Procedure

A) Harvesting Cells

  • Collect cells grown on YESCA medium
  • Centrifuge at 6,000 × g for 10 min at 4 °C
  • Resuspend pellet in cold PBS

B) Separation of Curli Fibers

  • Vortex vigorously for 5–10 min
  • Centrifuge at 8,000 rpm for 10 min at 4 °C
  • Collect supernatant containing curli fibers

C) Detergent and Enzymatic Treatment

  • Add SDS, DNase I, RNase A, and Proteinase K
  • Incubate at 80 °C for 10–15 min

D) Ultracentrifugation and Washing

  • Ultracentrifuge at 100,000 × g for 30–60 min
  • Wash pellet 2–3 times with PBS or water

Lysine Testing Using Coomassie Brilliant Blue

Aim

To detect surface-exposed lysine tags on curli fibers using Coomassie Brilliant Blue.

Principle

Coomassie Brilliant Blue G-250 binds electrostatically to exposed positive charges, producing stronger blue coloration in lysine-tagged samples compared to untagged controls.

Reagents

  • Coomassie Brilliant Blue G-250: 0.1 g (for 100 mL staining solution)​.
  • Methanol (absolute): 40 mL​
  • Glacial acetic acid: 10 mL​
  • Deionized water: 50 mL (to final volume)​
  • Destain buffer components: 40% methanol, 10% acetic acid in water​.

Equipment

  • YESCA media: Yeast extract (5 g/L), casamino acids (10 g/L) for curli induction​.
  • Petri dishes or 6-well plates: For growing biofilms/pellicles​.
  • Centrifuge tubes: 1.5-15 mL polypropylene​
  • Sonicator or vortex mixer: Fiber extraction from biofilms​
  • PBS (pH 7.4): 10 mM phosphate, 150 mM NaCl​
  • Shaker/rocker: Room temperature incubation
  • Staining trays/dishes: Glass or plastic (~10 cm diameter)
  • Pipettes: 1-10 mL with tips
  • Imaging: Dissecting microscope or smartphone camera
  • Controls: Wild-type CsgA strain (positive), ΔcsgA strain (negative)

Preparation

  • Dissolve 0.1 g Coomassie Brilliant Blue G-250 in 40 mL methanol, 10 mL glacial acetic acid, and 50 mL deionized H₂O to make 100 mL staining solution (0.1% w/v); filter if cloudy.
  • Prepare a destain buffer (40% methanol, 10% acetic acid in H2O). Grow E. coli biofilms on YESCA agar or in static liquid culture (37°C → 26°C induction), harvest pellicles or purify fibers via sonication/centrifugation (10,000g, 10 min) in PBS.

Procedure

  • Place biofilms (on membrane filters) or pelleted fibers (~1-5 mg) in a small dish; add enough staining solution to cover (5-10 mL).
  • Incubate 10-30 min at room temp with gentle shaking—heat to 50°C (water bath) for 5 min to accelerate if needed.
  • Decant stain, rinse twice with 5 mL destain buffer (1 min each), then H2O (2x).
  • Air-dry and observe both the lysine-tagged curli stains and wild-type curli.

Interpretation

Intense blue staining indicates lysine-tagged fibers; faint staining indicates absence of exposed lysine tags.

Preparation of MXene Nanosheets

Aim

The aim of this procedure is to prepare single- or few-layer MXene (Ti3C2Tx) nanosheets from multilayer MXene powder. These nanosheets will be used for future integration with engineered curli fibers in a composite water filtration system.

Materials

  • Multilayer Ti3C2Tx MXene powder
  • Intercalation agents (e.g., lithium-based salts or organic hydroxides, as described in the literature)
  • Deionized water or an appropriate aqueous solvent
  • Standard laboratory equipment for mixing, delamination, and separation

Procedure

MXene nanosheets will be prepared from multilayer Ti3C2Tx powder through a sequence of intercalation, delamination, and collection steps, following previously reported methods.

First, the multilayer MXene powder will undergo intercalation, where small ions or molecules are introduced between stacked MXene layers to increase interlayer spacing and weaken interlayer interactions. Next, the intercalated material will be delaminated using mechanical agitation methods to separate the multilayer structure into individual or few-layer MXene nanosheets.

Finally, the resulting nanosheets will be collected and separated from any unexfoliated material, yielding a dispersion enriched in delaminated MXene nanosheets. These nanosheets will be used in subsequent experiments involving composite formation with engineered curli fibers.

Expected Outcome

This workflow is expected to produce negatively charged MXene nanosheets that can interact favorably with lysine-functionalized curli fibers. The resulting material should be suitable for use in composite water purification systems.

Preparation of Chitosan Beads

Chitosan is soluble in dilute acidic solutions due to protonation of its amine groups but becomes insoluble in alkaline conditions. Dropping a chitosan solution into a basic coagulation bath results in instantaneous gelation, forming spherical chitosan beads.

Materials Needed

  • Chitosan powder (medium to high molecular weight)
  • Glacial or laboratory-grade acetic acid
  • Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)
  • Distilled water
  • Magnetic stirrer
  • Syringe, micropipette, or peristaltic pump
  • Beaker
  • pH paper or pH meter

Procedure

1. Preparation of Chitosan Solution

Prepare a 1–2% (v/v) acetic acid solution by diluting acetic acid with distilled water. Slowly add 1–2% (w/v) chitosan powder to the acetic acid solution while stirring continuously.

For example, dissolve 1 g of chitosan in 100 mL of acetic acid solution. Stir continuously for 6–12 hours until the chitosan is fully dissolved, forming a clear to slightly viscous solution.

Optional removal of air bubbles can be performed by resting the solution or applying mild vacuum.

2. Coagulation Bath Preparation

Prepare a 1–2 M NaOH solution in distilled water. Transfer the solution into a beaker and maintain gentle stirring.

3. Beading

Load the chitosan solution into a syringe, micropipette, or peristaltic pump. Dispense the solution dropwise into the NaOH bath from a height of approximately 5–10 cm. Chitosan beads will immediately form and solidify upon contact with the alkaline solution.

Bead Size Control Parameters:

  • Smaller dispensing tip results in smaller beads
  • Faster stirring produces more spherical beads
  • Higher solution viscosity leads to larger bead size

4. Hardening

Allow the beads to remain in the NaOH solution for 30–60 minutes to ensure complete gelation.

5. Washing

Collect the beads by filtration or decanting and wash repeatedly with distilled water until the wash solution reaches neutral pH (~7).
