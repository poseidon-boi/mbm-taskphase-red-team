Human Practices

Introduction

From the beginning of our project, we ensured it was grounded in values that go beyond technical success. We took responsibility, sustainability, and usability as core principles that would not only shape our design but also help us interact with the communities affected by the problem. With the widespread use of organochlorine pesticides (OCPs) and their persistent nature as contaminants in water, We Sought a solution that is both biologically informed and socially responsible. To understand the problem from the perspective of those affected, we plan to engage with stakeholders and IHP contacts, including environmental scientists, pesticide researchers, water treatment professionals, and professors who interact with these contaminants. These constraints helped us find where existing solutions fall short and clarify the practical constraints that any real-world technology must address. Throughout our project, we ensured our design was environmentally safe and user-friendly. We also plan to assess the potential risks of deploying biological systems in water treatment. Through discussions and risk assessments, through research papers, we developed a bio-based filter concept that is effective, scalable, and ethically sound. By viewing Human Practices not just as an add-on but as a key framework, we aim to improve our solution through feedback from relevant communities and experts. As a result, our solution is grounded in scientific rigor, informed by stakeholder needs, and aligned with the environmental and ethical expectations of real-world use.

Stakeholders

Primary: Farmers

Even though many organochlorine pesticides (OCPs), such as DDT, HCH, endosulfan, aldrin, and dieldrin, have been banned or restricted, they persist and continue to be detected in irrigation water and groundwater in agricultural regions due to their long-term environmental stability and historical use. In the irrigation water samples collected near agricultural fields in and around Jaipur, OCP residues were found in open-well and tube-well sources, with compounds such as HCH, endosulfan, and dieldrin detected at concentrations ranging from 2-16 ppb (Sharma & Khan, 2009). Similarly, monitoring of groundwater used for irrigation in and around Delhi revealed the presence of multiple organochlorine insecticides in water samples, even though levels were below WHO limits, indicating that these persistent pesticides enter water systems used for agricultural purposes (Mukherjee & Gopal, 2002).

Contact

We had an interactive session with Mr. Krishnakumar Menon, a farmer who maintains and cultivates his land in Varavoor, Thrissur district, Kerala. He mainly cultivates bananas, coconuts, arica nuts, and seasonal crops like turmeric. We learned about the application of different pesticides for different crops and about how agricultural regulations and rules are not uniform across India. He wasn't aware of OCPs, which gave us an opportunity to raise awareness on the issue and our proposed solution. He was fascinated by our solution and is looking forward to the success of our project.

Secondary: Public

We, as the public, are also to some extent responsible for the contamination of water by OCPs. The products we use, such as Lindane shampoo or lotion, have been used as topical treatments for head lice and scabies in humans. Lindane (γ-hexachlorocyclohexane) is an organochlorine pesticide that was originally developed as an agricultural insecticide and later adapted for medical use in shampoos and lotions (Humans, 2018; Nolan et al., 2012). Though their use has reduced due to safety concerns and regulatory restrictions, residues and outdated formulations can persist in supply chains, causing risks to human dermal and environmental exposure.

Integrated Human Practices (IHP)

We are aware that new approaches alone cannot be effective without understanding their practical contexts. As we move forward with our project, we intend to engage with a vast range of stakeholders, ranging from farmers to professionals and professors, and so on, considering their feedback and implementing it to improve our project. The motive behind such interactions not only contributes to influencing our technical approach but also helps in gaining insights regarding possible limitations in implementation, which we may not have taken into account. By considering their perspectives in our project, we try to assess our ideas and, in turn, improve our project to make it more feasible.
