Engineering

Design–Build–Test–Learn (DBTL): Engineering Through Iteration

Our study employed the Design–Build–Test–Learn (DBTL) methodology, focusing primarily on iterative design refinement, supporting evidence, and real-world applicability (Duraj-Thatte et al., 2019). Rather than converging on a single solution, multiple design strategies were evaluated and refined based on feasibility and literature support.

Iteration 1: SpyTag–SpyCatcher as a Modular Molecular Linker

Design

A modular biomolecular linker based on the SpyTag–SpyCatcher system was designed for immobilizing cyclodextrins (CDs) onto protein scaffolds to enable the capture of organochlorine pesticides (Zakeri et al., 2012). The strong covalent bond formed between SpyTag and SpyCatcher was leveraged to exploit the hydrophobic cavities of CDs for OCP binding.

Build

  • SpyCatcher–protein scaffold fusion
  • SpyTag–cyclodextrin conjugate (chemical linkage or peptide extension)
  • Expression system: E. coli for recombinant fusion protein production

Test

Dry-lab testing was not pursued due to a lack of literature support for Spy-linked cyclodextrin binding to OCPs. Existing studies did not provide evidence for successful cyclodextrin conjugation using the SpyTag–SpyCatcher system.

Learn

The absence of experimental and theoretical validation indicated that OCP capture via Spy-linked CDs was not feasible. Consequently, this design was discontinued at the conceptual stage to prioritize more robust approaches.

Iteration 2: Curli Fibres as a Programmable Scaffold

Design

Curli fibres were selected as the scaffold due to their mechanical robustness and programmable surface chemistry (Chapman et al., 2002; Duraj-Thatte et al., 2016). The curli subunit protein was engineered to include lysine residues to facilitate linker attachment and downstream cyclodextrin binding.

Build

Curli fibres were modified to present surface-exposed lysine residues without disrupting fibre assembly. These fibres were chemically conjugated with β-cyclodextrins using lysine-reactive chemistry, producing a functionalized biofilm-based scaffold.

Test

Structural integrity, adsorption potential, and cyclodextrin–OCP interactions were assessed (Saokham et al., 2018; Sharma et al., 2019). Difficulty in achieving stable linker attachment limited pollutant capture efficiency.

Learn

While curli fibres proved to be an effective scaffold, β-cyclodextrins were unsuitable as binding agents due to ineffective attachment and poor OCP interaction. This prompted exploration of alternative binding strategies.

Iteration 3: Functionalised Cyclodextrins and Chemical Coupling

Design

Two strategies were evaluated to enhance OCP binding. Track A utilized EDC-mediated covalent attachment of carboxymethyl-β-cyclodextrin, exploiting improved hydrophobic affinity (Saokham et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2017). Track B explored click chemistry approaches including SPAAC and maleimide–thiol coupling on cysteine-engineered curli fibres.

Build

EDC/NHS chemistry successfully enabled CD attachment. Click chemistry approaches, including azide-functionalized CDs and maleimide-modified curli fibres, were attempted but found impractical due to complexity and reagent requirements (Sinkkonen & Paasivirta, 2000; Sharma et al., 2019).

Test

Binding assays revealed insufficient adsorption for wastewater treatment, particularly in complex matrices containing humic substances and suspended solids. Although click chemistry promised specificity, it was cost-prohibitive and time-intensive (Keeble et al., 2017; SpyTag–SpyCatcher protein hydrogels, 2016).

Learn

Achieving high binding affinity, low synthesis cost, and real-world functionality simultaneously proved unfeasible. Cyclodextrin-based systems could not reach the required degradation thresholds, motivating a shift toward materials-based solutions.

Final Design: Material-Driven Adsorption With Biological Structuring

Design

The final strategy focuses on an eco-friendly, scalable bio-composite filter for effective adsorption of organochlorine pesticides, transitioning from molecular capture to materials-driven adsorption.

Material Basis

  • Chitosan: Biodegradable polysaccharide with proven OCP adsorption capacity (Behrooznia & Nourmohammadi, 2024; Huang et al., 2011)
  • MXene nanosheets: High surface-area materials with tunable adsorption properties (Rasool et al., 2020;Hansen et al., 2024)
  • Lysine-tagged curli fibres for structural reinforcement (Duraj-Thatte et al., 2019)

Build

Composite beads or layered matrices will be synthesized using chitosan and MXenes. Engineered E. coli will secrete curli fibres to reinforce the composite surface, forming a dual-layer filtration prototype.

Test

Docking studies showed promising interactions between lysine-tagged curli fibres and OCPs. Wet-lab validation will involve measuring OCP concentrations pre- and post-filtration using solid-phase microextraction (SPME).

Learn

Chitosan–MXene nanocomposites offer sustainable adsorption with enhanced durability. Future work will focus on MXene surface modification, regeneration cycles, and field-scale deployment.
